Week 3 Lecture 1 - Regular expressions

Aug 8, 2018  

Groupings

Suppose we have the disjunction of a number of regular expressions, and we wish to further match another disjunction of other regular expressions. We can write this by grouping the disjunctions within brackets, then concatenating the “grouped” disjunctions.

\[ (ab \cup ba)(e \cup g) \Rightarrow { abe, abg, bae, bag } \]

Infinite and finite languages

It’s straightforward to show that all languages with a finite number of words are representable as regular expressions since the disjunction of all words in the language is a valid and finite regular expression that captures the language. For infinite languages we cannot apply this reasoning as the disjunction would be of infinite length and therefore not a regular expression.

Kleene star

The Kleene Star is used to match a regular expression zero or more times. The Kleene Star is one such tool that allows us to represent some infinite languages with regular expressions.

(More) formal definition of a regular expression

A regular expression can be defined by the following rules

  1. \(\epsilon\) and \(\varnothing\) are regular expressions
  2. All letters in the alphabet are regular expressions
  3. If \(R\) and \(S\) are regular expressions then so are

i. \(( R)\) ii. \(RS\) iii. \(R \cup S\) iv. \(R*\)

A regular language is a language that can be described by a regular expression. If a word \(w\) belongs to the language and is described by a regular expression \(R\) then we say that \(w\) is matched by \(R\).

Questions to think about

Is the set of all English words a regular language?

Since there are a finite number of words in the English language (for example the Oxford English dictionary), we can just consider the disjunction of all those words. Since this is finite it is a valid regular expression that describes the language. Therefore English is a regular language. What may be more interesting is if there exists a shorter non-trivial regular expression that also describes English.

Is \(PALINDROMES\) a regular language?

Since we are unable to “remember” earlier parts of the expression we have matched, there are an infinite number of possible palindromes so we cannot exhaustively list them all and take the disjunction. There cannot exist a single regular expression that describes \(PALINDROMES\) therefore it is not a regular language.

Is \(DOUBLEWORD\) a regular language?

Similar to the case with \(PALINDROMES\), we cannot match already captured symbols and as a result there does not exist a non-trivial regular expression. Since \(DOUBLEWORD\) is also infinite, there is no regular expression that describes it and therefore it is also not a regular language.
