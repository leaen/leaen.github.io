  1. CAPM

    market portfolio \(M\) is a portfolio which given a set of investments, the ideal combination of which will obtain an expected return \(E(R_M)\) with standard deviation of returns \(\sigma_M\). We should note that \(M\) is the portfolio that lives the furthest "north-west" on the efficient frontier plot.

    e form a relationship between the returns of an investment and the returns of a market portfolio. This relationship is typically a best-fit linear model in the form of

    $ R = α + β RM + ε $$

    e note that there are two stochastic components in this model, the first \(\beta R_M\) being labelled as the systematic risk of the investment and a second \(\epsilon\), known as the nonsystematic risk of the investment.

    or investors with large portfolios, under the assumption that the \(\epsilon\) for each investment is independent, nonsystematic risk is quickly diversified away.

    or higher levels of systematic risk, an investor should also require a higher level of return to account for this uncertainty in the value of the investment. In an investment with no systematic risk (\(\beta = 0\)) we should expect to receive the risk-free rate \(R_F\). When we have equal systematic risk to the market (\(\beta = 1\)) we should expect market returns. Generally,

    $ E(R) = RF + β [E(RM) - RF] $$

    ere we see that the excess expected return over the risk-free rate required is equal to \(\beta\) times the excess return over the market portfolio. Here \(\beta\) is known as the beta of the investment, and represents the sensitivity of the return from the investment to the returns from the market portfolio.

    he closed form of beta (the coefficient in a linear regression of market returns on investment returns) is

    $ β = ρ × \frac{\sigma}{\sigma_M} $$

    here \(\rho\) gives the correlation between the return from the investment and the return from the market portfolio.

  2. CAPM assumptions

    . We only care about the expected return and the standard deviation of returns and not other characteristics of the distribution (skewness, excess kurtosis).

    . We have assumed that the nonsystematic risk of each investment is independent and will be diversified in larger portfolio. This may not necessarily be the case, take the example of a set of investments within the automotive sector. If the price of an input to automotive manufacturing rises then returns on all automotive stocks will be impact, leading to correlation in returns that is not captured by the movements of the entire market.

    . Investors have the same time-horizon for their investments and are all considering each investment at the present.

    . Investors are able to borrow and lend at the risk-free rate \(R_F\) in order to achieve portfolios with \(\beta > 1\) or \(\beta < 1\) (see figure 1.4).

    . We are not considering tax

    . Investors all have the same estimates of returns of investments, standard deviation, correlations. This is known as homogeneous expectations.
