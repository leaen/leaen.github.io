1.1 Lecture 1

Systematic risk is the risk that the whole market is correlated. An example of this is the financial crisis in which the majority of stocks are positively correlated and when one falls, they all fall. This refers to system-wide risk, and not risk affecting a singular firm (called idiosyncratic risk).

1.2 Lecture 2

1.2.1 Tangent-like portfolios

Suppose that we have two portfolios, one risk-free and one a tangent portfolio. If we design a portfolio that is composed between these two, then we have a rate \(r_P\) equal to

\[ r_P = (1-w) r_f + w r_T \]

This allows us to replication any portfolio on the tangent line to the mean-variance curve (?).

We define the market portfolio as the portfolio of all \(N\) stocks in the economy with weights

\[ w_i = \frac{V_i}{V_1 + \ldots + V_n} \]

1.2.2 Understanding CAPM

The reason that we only really look at the covariance of a new asset when adding it to the market portfolio is since the variance of the new portfolio can be calculated as

\[ Var[r_p + a_i(r_i - r_0)] = Var[r_p] + a_i^2 \sigma_i^2 + 2 a_i Cov(r_i, r_p) \]

where we see the weight \(a_i\) dominates the term with \(a_i^2\) since \(a_i < 1\) and is typically less than 1%.

1.2.3 Why buy negative beta?

Why would you buy an asset with a smaller expected return than the risk-free rate? Well one reason is that if that asset has negative beta, then it acts as a hedge against a market-wide fall, meaning that if the economy as a whole tanks, then the asset rises. An example of something with negative beta would be a put option on S&P 500.

1.2.4 Types of diversification

So far, we have looked at cross-sectional diversification meaning that we have looked at composing a portfolio consisting of many many assets. We may also look to diversify and avoid autocorrelation between the returns of assets over time, known as time-diversification.

1.3 Lecture 3

1.3.1 Forward contracts

  1. Explanation

    An agreement to purchase an asset at a particular price at a future date. We note in particular that there is an obligation to trade (as opposed to options).

    Most forward contracts are traded OTC as they are normally highly customised and specific. No money changes hands at the inception of the contract.

    A common example of a forward contract would be dealing with payments with foreign firms. To protect against fluctuations in exchange rates, a soybean producer in China may enter a contract with a US importer to trade $1 million USD at a particular price in one year. This protects both parties from the exchange rate.

  2. Payoffs

    Considering a forward contract with price \(F\) that matures at time \(T\). Let \(S_T\) be the final price of the underlying at time \(T\). The gain of the buyer of the contract at maturity is \(S_T - F\), and the sellers gain is \(-(S_T - F)\).

    If \(S_T < F\) then the buyer loses and the seller gains, if \(S_T > F\) then the buyer gains and the seller loses.

  3. Pricing

    To derive the price of the forward contract, we consider the portfolio where

    • We borrow one share of stock
    • Borrow \(F/(1+r)^T\) USD

    Since this portfolio matches the payouts given by the forward contract, by the principle of no-arbitrage pricing, the value of the forward at \(t=0\) is given by

    \[ V = S - \frac{F}{(1+r)^T} \]

    Therefore, since we assume that there is no cost to enter the contract (\(V=0\)), then we can derive the forward price by

    \[ F = S \times (1+r)^T \]

  4. Futures contracts

    A futures contract is an exchange traded forward contract, typically implying that the contract is more standardised. This reduces counterparty risk, in large part since the contracts are marked to market in a margin account.

1.3.2 Interest rate swaps

Two parties will swap cashflows. One party pays a fixed rate \(c\) on a notional amount \(N\) up until a time \(T\). The other party pays a floating rate \(r_n(t)\).

A common floating rate used is the LIBOR, which is the average rate at which London banks are willing to lend and borrow money from each other.

1.3.3 Delta

The delta of a portfolio is the partial derivative of the price of the portfolio with respect to the spot price.

\[ \Delta = \frac{\partial P}{\partial S} \]

This gives the sensitivity of the portfolio to changes in some underlying spot price for which the portfolio is constructed upon. Using elementary calculus, if we let \(P(s)\) be the price of the portfolio as a function of the underlying then we see that

\[ P(s+h) - P(s) \approx \Delta \times h \]

for small \(h\). This shows that investments with large \(\Delta\) tend to be more sensitive to changes in the underlying price while investments with low \(\Delta\) tend to be less sensitive.

To hedge investments with non-linear \(\Delta\), there is a need to frequently rebalance to preserve delta neutrality.

1.4.1 Put-call parity

To create a relationship between the price of European calls and puts, we create the following portfolio.

Portfolio Cash at \(t\) \(S_T \leq K\) \(S_T > K\)
Short call \(c\) \(0\) \(-(S_T-K)\)
Long put \(-p\) \(K-S_T\) \(0\)
Long stock \(-S_t\) \(S_T\) \(S_T\)
Borrow \(KB(t, T)\) \(-K\) \(-K\)
Total \(c - p - S_t + KB(t,T)\) \(0\) \(0\)

The no arbitrage assumption implies then that the price of this portfolio must be equal to $0. Therefore,

\begin{array}{rcl} c - p - S_t + KB(t, T) &=& 0 \\ \Rightarrow c &=& p + S_t - KB(t, T) \\ \end{array}

which is known as put-call parity. In some cases however, this relationship may be violated. When this occurs, there is a possibility to make a risk-free profit. If we consider that any rational investor would take an opportunity to make a non-negative amount of money with non-zero probability, these violations are quickly arbitraged away.

1.4.2 Black-Scholes-Merton

Let \(\Pi_t\) be the value of a portfolio composed of one long call and one short position in the underlying of size \(\Delta\). Consider a small change in the value of the portfolio overtime,

\[ \Pi_{t+dt} - \Pi_t \approx (\text{some riskless return}) dt + \frac{\partial C}{\partial S} \times (S_{t+dt} - S_t) - \Delta \times (S_{t+dt} - S_t) \]

We want to pick \(\Delta\) such that the risky component of the the change in \(\Pi\) is eliminated. Therefore we set \(\Delta = \frac{\partial C}{\partial S}\). Now since the portfolio is riskless, the rate of return must be equal to the risk-free rate.

This gives a differential equation which we can solve to get

\begin{array}{rcl} c &=& S \mathcal{N}(d_1) - Ke^{r(T-t)}\mathcal{N}(d_2) \\ d_1 &=& \frac{ln(\frac{S}{K}) + (r + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2)(T-t)}{\sigma \sqrt{T-t}} \\ d_2 &=& d_1 - \sigma \sqrt{T-t} \\ p &=& Ke^{-r(T-t)}\mathcal{N}(-d_2) - S \mathcal{N}(-d_1) \\ \end{array}

When the spot price goes up, the call moves further into the money and the put moves further away from the money. 1.4.5 Greeks - Delta \((\Delta)\)

Delta or \(\Delta\) measures the sensitivity of the option to the underlying price. It gives the size of the hedge position in the underlying we can take in order to hedge changes in the price of the underlying.

Delta gives the number of units of underlying we should purchase in order to construct a locally riskless portfolio.

We can use a taylor series expansion to approximate delta.

Typically investors will prefer investments with relatively low delta (since they don't need to hedge as much) and with unchanging delta (so they don't need to readjust their hedge, and incur transaction fees).

For calls, delta is positive, meaning that a rise in the price of the underlying makes the call more valuable, while a drop makes it less valuable. The reverse is true for puts since a rise in the price of the underlying moves the put further from the money, lowering its value.

1.4.6 Greeks - Gamma \((\Gamma)\)

Gamma represents the change in delta with respect to a change in the price of the underlying. In other words, gamma is equal to the second partial derivative of the price of the option with respect to the price of the underlying.

\[ \Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial S^2} \]

Investors can hedge gamma by trading volatility derivatives (e.g. options on VIX). A gamma of 0 means that delta is unchanging, and that a delta-hedged portfolio will not need to be readjusted in order to maintain delta-hedged after a move in the price of the underlying.

1.4.7 Greeks - Vega \((V)\)

Vega is the first partial derivative of option price with respect to volatility of the underlying.

\[ V = \frac{\partial P}{\partial \sigma} \]

We can hedge vega with exotic derivatives.

1.4.8 Greeks - Rho \((\rho)\)

Rho (\(\rho\)) is the first partial derivative of option price with respect to the riskfree rate \(r\).

\[ \rho = \frac{\partial P}{\partial r} \]

1.4.9 Greeks - Theta \((\Theta)\)

Theta ($θ) is the first partial derivative of option price with respect to time.

\[ \theta = \frac{\partial P}{\partial t} \]

Theta measures the rate at which options lose their value over time. An option far from expiration has low theta and an option near expiration will have high theta.

1.5.1 Bonds

Bonds are a financial contract consisting of many parts. In essence, they are give a cashflow over a period of time from one party to another. Almost always, at the date of maturity of the bond a face value is paid from the issuer to the holder of the bond. Often, there are payments at regular intervals in the interim before maturity, called coupons. The coupon rate is the percent of face value paid in each coupon. A bond without coupons is called a zero coupon bond. Issuers are typically governments or corporations who wish to raise debt.

Risk in bonds typically comes from price risk and default risk. Price formula Yield to maturity

1.5.2 Spot rates

Let \(\delta_t\) be the discount factor for a maturity date \(t\). This is the price of a zero-coupon bond that will pay $1 at time \(t\) in the future.

We can calculate the price of \(\delta_t\) by discrete or continuous compounding, for example

\begin{array}{rcl} B(0,t) &=& \delta_t = e^{-r_t t} \\ B(0,t) &=& \delta_t = \frac{1}{(1 + R_t)^t} \\ \end{array}

If we solve this for \(R_t\), the yield on a zero-coupon bond expiring at time \(t\) with annual compounding, we find

\[ R_t = \left (\frac{1}{\delta_t}\right)^{1/t} - 1 \]

We can interpret this as the risk-free rate of return over a given time period. Considering these spot rates as a function of time, we arrive at the yield curve, which specifies the risk-free rate for a given future period.

1.5.3 Bond pricing

Consider an arbitrary cashflow \((c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_T)\). One simple method of calculating the current price of such a cashflow (perhaps from a bond) is by discounting each cashflow to the present value

\[ P = \sum_{t=1}^T \frac{c_t}{(1+R_t)^t} \]

where \(R_t\) is the discretely compounded spot rate (not the yield on a \(t\) - year coupon bond (?). Recognising \(\delta_t\), we can rearrange to get

\[ P = \sum_{t=1}^T \delta_t c_t = \delta_1 c_1 + \delta_2 c_2 + \ldots + \delta_T c_T \]

which links the price of an arbitrary cashflow to the zero-coupon rates.

1.5.6 Yield to maturity

Yield to maturity (YTM) is a rate \(y\) such that

\[ P = \sum_{t=1}^T \frac{c_t}{(1+y)^t} \]

We should note that the yield to maturity is not equal to the total return of the bond.

1.5.7 Hedging bond risk

One of the biggest risks to holding bonds is fluctuations in interest rates. We can see that as interest rates rise, bond prices fall as the future cashflows are more heavily discounted. For this reason, we may be interested in the sensitivity of a bond's price to changes in the interest rate. Let \(P = \sum_{i=1}^N c_i e_i e^{-rt_i}\)

Using taylor's expansion, a first order approximation of the derivative with respect to \(r\) is given by

\[ \frac{dP}{dr} = - \sum_{i=1}^N t_i c_i e^{-rt_i} \]

The modified duration is the percentage change in the bond price for a one percent change in interest rates. Macaulay duration gives a weighted average of the time at which you receive coupon payments.

1.5.8 Bond duration and convexity

Duration is used to approximate the change in the bond price in response to a change in the interest rate. Duration is equal to the line tangent to the price-yield curve at the yield \(\bar{y}\). However, when using this linear tangent to the curve, there is an amount of error when calculating this predicted change.

We can improve this estimate by looking at the convexity of the bond. Convexity is the second derivative of dollar change.

1.6.2 Portfolio insurance

One of the simplest methods of protecting a portfolio against a fall in prices is to purchase a put option. However in some cases, put options are illiquid or not available for the required periods. We can replicate a desired put option with dynamic hedging. Recall that by definition

\[ \Delta = \frac{\partial P}{\partial S} = \frac{p' -p}{S' - S} \]

therefore, the chanages in the portfolios on page 179 are equal, or

\[ p' - p \approx S' \Delta - S \Delta \]

After a price move, if \(\Gamma > 0\), then we will need to readjust our \(\Delta\) hedge. To rebalance, we want to set our portfolios overall \(\Delta\) to match that of the theoretical put option. Therefore after a price change we need to sell \(-(\Delta' - \Delta)\) units of stock. We continue this process of adjusting our hedge until expiry.

If many investors replicate this strategy then it may create a feedback effect, in which as the price falls, investors continue to sell more and more stock, further pushing the price downward. This creates an endogenous risk since larger jumps in price might occur.

Monday October 19, 1987. Brady commission.

In this case, since prices moved too quickly, synthetic or dynamically replicated positions were not able to replicate a real put option (Grossman, 1988).

1.6.4 Hedging mortgage-backed securities

The risk of prepayment is the risk that mortgage holders will repay their loans sooner than expected. Consider the case in which an indvidiual takes out a mortgage at 5%, and then interest rates fall to 3%. The mortgage holder may then take a loan out at a rate of 3% and pay off the original loan. The duration of the original loan has now fallen substantially.

Mortgage lenders buy treasury bonds in order to hedge aggregate interest rate risk. This may again cause a feedback loop generating endogenous risk within the market as treasury bond prices continue to get pushed up and interest rates fall as a result.

We also note that this is the opposite of convential bonds, in which when interest rates rise, duration falls.

1.6.8 Noise trader risk

We may propose that there are two groups of investors

  1. Rational arbitrageurs
  2. Noise traders

It is supposed that while rational arbitrageurs understand the true value of a particular security, uninformed investors may hold the price down or suppress it to the point where the rational investor may need to liquidate their own position.

We can assume that \(\mu\) gives the proportion of noise investors in the market. suppose that noise traders misperceive the expected price of a risky asset \(u\) by

\[ \rho_t \sim \text{i.i.d. } \mathcal{N}(\rho^*, \sigma^2) \]

with a utility function

\[ U_t = - \text{exp}(-2 \gamma w_t) \]

then prices are given by

\[ p_t = 1 + \frac{\mu(\rho_t - \rho^*)}{1+r} + \frac{\mu \rho^*}{r} - 2 \gamma \frac{\mu^2 \sigma^2}{r(1+r)^2} \]

where \(r\) is the riskless interest rate of a safe security \(s\). This shows that some mispriced assets may be avoided even though they do not trade at fair price since noise traders may suppress the price, and skew the trade.

1.7 Lecture 7

1.7.1 Risk measures

  • Volatility
  • Value at Risk
  • Expected shortfall or tail loss

Since financial returns often have negative skewness, investor preferences are rarely a function of just mean and variance of returns. As a result volatility is incomplete.

VaR is a quantile on the distribution of profit and loss. Mathematically,

\[ Pr[q \leq -\text{VaR}(p)] = p = \int_{- \infty}^{-\text{VaR}(p)} f(q)dq \]

When discussing VaR, it is also crucial to also specify a holding period and a confidence level.

1.7.2 Coherent risk measures

  • Montonicity

\[ X \leq Y \Rightarrow \psi(X) \geq \psi(Y) \]

An asset \(X\) with no better returns than an asset \(Y\) has at least as much risk as \(Y\).

  • Subadditivity

\[ \psi(X+Y) \leq \psi(X) + \psi(Y) \]

Risk of holding two assets together is no worse than holding each separately. Volatility is sub-additive.

  • Positive homogeneity

\[ \psi(cX) = c\psi(X), c > 0\]

If we increase the size of \(X\), then the risk increases in the same proportion.

  • Translation invariance

\[ \psi(X+c) = \psi(X) - c \]

If the payoff increases by a particular amount \(c\) then the risk must fall by the same amount.

1.7.3 Expected shortfall

Expected shortfall or ES is the expected loss conditional on losses exceeding VaR. Mathematically,

\[ ES = \mathbb{E}[\text{loss}|\text{loss} \geq VaR(p)] = \frac{\int_{-\infty}^{-\text{VaR}(p)} qf(q)dq}{\int_{-\infty}^{-\text{VaR}(p)} f(q)dq} = - \frac{1}{p} \int_{-\infty}^{-\text{VaR}(p)} qf(q)dq \]

1.7.4 Conditional variance

We make the assumption that \(r_t = log(P_t / P_{t-1}) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_t^2)\). Further, we make the assumption that the variance at time \(t\) is equal to a function of all previous returns, or

\[ \sigma_t^2 = h_t = f(r_1, \ldots, r_{t-1}) \]

Lastly, residuals

\[ z_t = \frac{r_t}{\sqrt{h_t}} \]

3.1 Chapter 1 - Introduction

3.1.1 CAPM

A market portfolio \(M\) is a portfolio which given a set of investments, the ideal combination of which will obtain an expected return \(E(R_M)\) with standard deviation of returns \(\sigma_M\). We should note that \(M\) is the portfolio that lives the furthest "north-west" on the efficient frontier plot.

We form a relationship between the returns of an investment and the returns of a market portfolio. This relationship is typically a best-fit linear model in the form of

\[ R = \alpha + \beta R_M + \epsilon \]

We note that there are two stochastic components in this model, the first \(\beta R_M\) being labelled as the systematic risk of the investment and a second \(\epsilon\), known as the nonsystematic risk of the investment.

For investors with large portfolios, under the assumption that the \(\epsilon\) for each investment is independent, nonsystematic risk is quickly diversified away.

For higher levels of systematic risk, an investor should also require a higher level of return to account for this uncertainty in the value of the investment. In an investment with no systematic risk (\(\beta = 0\)) we should expect to receive the risk-free rate \(R_F\). When we have equal systematic risk to the market (\(\beta = 1\)) we should expect market returns. Generally,

\[ E(R) = R_F + \beta [E(R_M) - R_F] \]

Here we see that the excess expected return over the risk-free rate required is equal to \(\beta\) times the excess return over the market portfolio. Here \(\beta\) is known as the beta of the investment, and represents the sensitivity of the return from the investment to the returns from the market portfolio.

The closed form of beta (the coefficient in a linear regression of market returns on investment returns) is

\[ \beta = \rho \times \frac{\sigma}{\sigma_M} \]

where \(\rho\) gives the correlation between the return from the investment and the return from the market portfolio.

3.1.2 CAPM assumptions

  1. We only care about the expected return and the standard deviation of returns and not other characteristics of the distribution (skewness, excess kurtosis).
  2. We have assumed that the nonsystematic risk of each investment is independent and will be diversified in larger portfolio. This may not necessarily be the case, take the example of a set of investments within the automotive sector. If the price of an input to automotive manufacturing rises then returns on all automotive stocks will be impact, leading to correlation in returns that is not captured by the movements of the entire market.
  3. Investors have the same time-horizon for their investments and are all considering each investment at the present.
  4. Investors are able to borrow and lend at the risk-free rate \(R_F\) in order to achieve portfolios with \(\beta > 1\) or \(\beta < 1\) (see figure 1.4).
  5. We are not considering tax
  6. Investors all have the same estimates of returns of investments, standard deviation, correlations. This is known as homogeneous expectations.
  7. All assets are tradable.

3.1.3 Alpha

Recall that CAPM relates the expected return for a portfolio given the expected market return and the beta of the investment. When analysing past performance, the markets return is fixed and therefore we can examine the excess return of an investment over what would be expected to have realised. Concretely, we have

\[ R = R_F + \beta (R_M - R_F) \]

and let \(\alpha\) be the excess return

\[ \alpha = R - R_F - \beta (R_M - R_F) \]

Therefore, when \(\alpha > 0\) then the investment has outperformed expectations (we might say the fund manager has generated alpha), and when \(\alpha < 0\) then the investment has underperformed.

3.2 Chapter 10 - Volatility

3.2.1 Indicators of market volatility

$VIX measures the implied volatility of 30-day options on S&P 500 and provides a commonly used benchmark for market volatility. Similarly, $VXN measures implied volatility for NASDAQ 100 and $VXD measures implied volatility for DJIA.

3.2.2 Distributions of returns in financial variables

The most common assumption is that returns are roughly normally distributed. However in financial markets we normally find that the distributions are more platykurtic than a normal distribution.

3.2.3 Power law

An alternative distribution that we may use the power law. Simply, we make the claim that

\[ Pr[v > x] = Kx^{-\alpha} \]

for some \(K, \alpha \in \mathbb{R}\). It is implied by this equation that \(ln(Pr[v > x]) = ln \, K - \alpha \, ln \, x\) holds also. Therefore we are able to estimate \(K\) and \(\alpha\) by linear regression of \(ln x\) on \(ln(Pr[v>x])\). Here we see a plot of the returns of an exchange rate shows an approximately linear relationship.

3.2.4 Estimating daily volatility

We can compute the standard deviation of returns by letting \(u_i\) be the continuously compounded return during day \(i\),

\[ u_i = ln \frac{S_i}{S_{i-1}} \]

and calculate \(\sigma_n\) as the standard deviation of the previous \(m\) returns

\[ \sigma_n^2 = \frac{1}{m-1} \sum_{i=1}^m (u_{n-i} - \bar{u})^2 \]

where \(\bar{u}\) is the mean of the returns

\[ \bar{u} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^m u_{n-i} \]

For risk management purposes, we normally make a few changes to this estimate

  1. Percentage changes

    Instead of using continuously compounding returns we prefer percent changes. Therefore we calculate \(u_i\) as

    \[ u_i = \frac{S_i - S_{i-1}}{S_{i-1}} \]

  2. Zero mean assumption

    We assume that the average return is equal to 0%. Therefore we set \(\bar{u}=0\).

  3. MLE instead of unbiased estimator

    Instead of using Bessel's adjustment we prefer to use the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) for the variance.

All together using these changes, we arrive at the esimator

\[ \sigma_n^2 = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m u_{n-i}^2 \]

3.2.5 Weighting schemes and the ARCH(\(m\)) model

Previously, we have given equal weighting to each of the past \(m\) observations. However, a more reasonable estimator by weight more recent observations more heavily since they provide a better indicator of the current volatility of the time series. Suppose we have weights \(\alpha_i\), then our estimate becomes

\[ \sigma_n^2 = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m \alpha_i u_{n-i}^2 \]

Sensible choices of the weights give \(\alpha_i < \alpha_j\) when \(i < j\) (older observations have smaller weights), and such that \(\sum_{i=1}^m \alpha_i = 1\). Further we may assume that there is some constant long run unobservable variance \(V_L\). We could choose to incorporate this long run variance by including it in our estimate, and adjusting the existing weights such that they still add to 1. Therefore we may choose the model

\[ \sigma_n^2 = \gamma V_L + \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m \alpha_i u_{n-i}^2 \] \[ \gamma + \sum_{i=1}^m \alpha_i = 1 \]

which is also known as an ARCH(\(m\)) model.

3.2.6 Exponentially weighted moving average (EWMA) model

Similar to the ARCH(\(m\)) model, the EWMA model gives weights to previous observations in a decreasing manner, however EWMA specifies that they decay in an exponential manner meaning that

\[ \alpha_i = \lambda \alpha_{i-1} \]

for \(\lambda \in [0,1]\). Some algebra will show our model is then

\[ \sigma_n^2 = \lambda \sigma_{n-1}^2 + (1-\lambda) u_{n-1}^2 \]

The choice of \(\lambda\) here determines how fast or slow the estimator is to react to large changes in volatility. For \(\lambda\) close to \(1\), greater weighting is put on the tail or older observations compared to a lower \(\lambda\) perhaps closer to \(0.5\).

One benefit of this model is it reduces the space overhead to constant. To calculate the next estimate of volatility, we simply need the previous estimate and the previous observation.

3.2.7 GARCH(1,1)

We could now propose a model that incorporates features of EWMA (exponentially decaying weights) as well as the long-run variance seen earlier in the ARCH(\(m\)) model. Here we see a GARCH(1,1) model

\[ \sigma_n^2 = \gamma V_L + \alpha u_{n-1}^2 + \beta \sigma_{n-1}^2 \]

with

\[ \gamma + \alpha + \beta = 1, \alpha + \beta < 1\]

More generally, a GARCH(\(p\),\(q\)) model uses the previous \(p\) observations of \(u^2\) and the previous \(q\) estimates of volatility. We can interpret \(\beta\) as the decay rate of the model (similar to \(\lambda\) in the EWMA model). In fact, EWMA is a special case of GARCH(1,1) with \(\gamma = 0\).

3.3 Chapter 12 - Value at Risk and Expected Shortfall

3.3.1 Value at Risk (VaR)

When using the VaR risk measure, we are essentially making statements in the form of,

We are \(X\) percent sure that we will not lose more than \(V\) over the next \(T\) periods.

Here, \(V\) is the VaR of the portfolio, and is itself a function of the confidence level \(X\) and the time horizon \(T\). This is relatively inuitive since we would expect to possibly lose different amounts over the next day compared to the next year, and similarly as our confidence level rises, we'd expect the VaR to rise as well.

3.3.2 Expected shortfall (ES)

One downside to VaR is its ability to be easily manipulated to misconstrue risk in a portfolio. For example a trader may construct a portfolio that wins $1 million 98.1% of the time, loses $1 million 1% of the time and loses $500 million 0.9% of the time. At a 1% confidence level the VaR is just $1 million despite the enourmous tail in the distribution.

A complement to VaR then is Expected shortfall (ES) or sometimes known as conditional VaR. ES is equal to the average loss conditional on that loses execeed VaR. Therefore ES is also a function of \(T\) and \(X\).

3.3.3 Coherent risk measures

  • Monotonicity
  • Translation invariance
  • Homogeneity
  • Subadditivity

See lecture 7 notes for more details. We should also note that VaR does not conform to subadditivity.

3.3.4 Spectral risk measures

Note that both VaR and ES are risk measures defined by a weighting of the loss distribution over particular percentiles. For VaR, the weighting is solely on the $X$th percentile, while with ES we have equal weighting for everything above the $X$th percentile.

More broadly a spectral risk measure is one which gives non-decreasing weights for higher and higher percentiles. By this definition, VaR is not a spectral risk measure since the weights for the $X$th percentile are greater than the percentiles above \(X\), but ES is a spectral risk measure. A useful result is that it can be shown that spectral risk measures are always coherent.

Another example of a spectral risk measure is the exponential spectral risk measure which gives a weight for the percent \(q\) that decay exponentially according to a parameter \(\gamma\).

\[ w_q = e^{-(1-q)/\gamma} \]

3.3.5 Autocorrelation in the returns of a portfolio

When calculating VaR for different periods of time, a simple assumption to make at the beginning of an analysis is that the returns of a portfolio each day have zero mean and are independent of each other. As a result of this asssumption, if we calculate the 1-day VaR to be \(V\) then the $T$-day VaR is simply equal to \(V \times \sqrt{T}\).

However this assumption is often violated when there is autocorrelation in the returns of the portfolio. This means that the returns today are not independent of yesterdays returns. As autocorrelation rises, the estimate of \(V \times \sqrt{T}\) becomes lower and lower than the fair prediction taking into account autocorrelation.

For simple first order autocorrelation, if \(\Delta P_i\) is the change in the value of the portfolio on day \(i\), then the standard deviation of \(\sum_{i=1}^T \Delta P_i\) is equal to

\[ \sigma \sqrt{T + 2(T-1)\rho + 2(T-2)\rho^2 + 2(T-3)\rho^3 + \ldots + 2 \rho^{T-1}} \]

3.3.6 Euler's theorem and risk budgeting

If \(x_i\) is the amount of capital allocated to a particular subportfolio then Euler's theorem states that

\[ V = \sum_{i=1}^M \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_i} x_i \]

and can imply a convenient allocation of capital to portfolios. This technique is known as risk budgeting.

6.1 Deciphering the Liquidity and Credit Crunch 2007-2008

6.1.1 CDS indices

$ITRX and $CDX were index funds that held a portfolio of CDSs.

6.1.2 Duration mismatch

Investors typically prefer to not have their funds locked up for longer periods of time. Since the term length of mortgages is typically multiple years or decades, the cashflows were restructured into securities with anywhere from 90-day to 1-year maturities.

As banks began to lend over the short term with notes and asset backed securities while also investing in longer term mortgages, they opened themselves up to funding liquidity risk if investor demand moved away from short term securities.

6.1.3 Structured products

The lure of structure products comes from a the proposal of a more efficient matching of lenders to borrowers. Structuring allows risk to be allocate to those who are willing to bare it, while safer cashflows are allocated to those with lower risk tolerances. By this method we are able to take a pool of mortgages and from them produce a series of tranches with ratings from AAA to BBB to unrated.

Most of the models assessing these structured products were built based off data collected from US housing markets in the period following WWII to present day. In this period there was no nation-wide housing contraction and default rates were historically low. This along with assumptions of low-correlation between default rates in different regions led to overly optimistic estimates on the performance of the mortgage backed securities.

6.1.4 Deterioration in quality of loans

As the supply of credit grew rapidly along with the growth in offerings of structured products, mortgage brokers began to hold mortgages for shorter and shorter periods of time. As a result, the mortgage brokers were not concerned with the chance of default, so long as the mortgage was sold on to another party before it would deafult. As a result of this, the quality of borrowers decreased substantially, mortgage brokers lent under riskier and riskier terms (no down payment, no proof of income).

With credit readily available and at such favourable rates, the housing market boomed as prices were driven up by individuals previously unable to borrow enough to purchase a property. In many cases, borrowers would purchase multiple properties, with house prices rising throughout 2004-2007 this never seemed to be an issue. These mortgages were labelled as "subprime".

(* Generates a tree of binomial moves and returns the bottom level *)
let rec generate_binomial_tree prices u d depth =
  let next_level prices =
    [(List.hd prices) *. u] @ List.map (fun x -> x *. d) prices
  in
  if depth <= 0 then
    prices
  else
    generate_binomial_tree (next_level prices) u d (depth-1)
in

(* Reduce the bottom level of the trees of
   values to the present value of the option *)
let rec reduce_prices prices r u d dt =
  (* Prices option one period back
     given the prices of the next period *)
  let reduce_pair pu pd r u d dt =
    let discount = exp (r *. dt) in
    let p = (discount -. d) /. (u -. d) in
    (p *. pu +. (1.-.p) *. pd) /. discount
  in

  (* Moves one period back, or one level up the tree *)
  let rec aux p acc =
    match p with
    | [] -> acc
    | [x] -> acc
    | x :: y :: tl ->
      let new_acc = acc @ [reduce_pair x y r u d dt] in
      aux ([y] @ tl) new_acc
  in

  match prices with
  | [] -> 0.
  | [x] -> x
  | tl -> reduce_prices (aux prices []) r u d dt
in

(* Uses binomial-tree pricing model to
   determine the value of a call and a put *)
let binomial_tree_price r s k weeks sigma =
  let depth = 250 in
  let dt = (weeks /. 52.) /. (float_of_int depth) in
  let u = exp (sigma *. sqrt dt) in
  let d = 1. /. u in

  let underlying_prices = generate_binomial_tree [s] u d depth in

  (* How to find max of two floats?? lol *)
  let max_of_floats x y =
    if x > y then x else y
  in

  let call_prices = List.map
      (fun x -> max_of_floats (x -. k) 0.)
      underlying_prices in

  let put_prices = List.map
      (fun x -> max_of_floats (k -. x) 0.)
      underlying_prices in

  let call_price = reduce_prices call_prices r u d dt in
  let put_price = reduce_prices put_prices r u d dt in

  (call_price, put_price)
in
